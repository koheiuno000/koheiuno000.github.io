#### Abstract
This study examines the long-term effect of preschool participation on children’s academic achievement and the moderating role of family socioeconomic status in the relationship between preschool participation and later academic achievement. The study uses data from Young Lives Ethiopia, which tracked children over a period of 15 years. Vocabulary, mathematics, and reading scores are used as indicators of academic achievement.

Ordinary least squares (OLS) regression is applied to estimate the association between preschool participation and later academic achievement. The results show that preschool participation is positively associated with later academic achievement, and the effect persists until age 15.

Overall, family socioeconomic status does not significantly moderate the relationship between preschool participation and later academic achievement. These findings suggest that investment in preschool programs is important for children from both low and high socioeconomic backgrounds to foster human capital accumulation. The study does not find strong evidence that preschool participation either reduces or widens socioeconomic gaps in later academic achievement.
